1. ATR — Average True Range

How much the stock moves in dollars per day on average. Calculated over the last 14 days.

Higher ATR = bigger daily swings. Lower ATR = calmer stock.

Use caseHow
Pick strikeSell options at least 1 to 2 ATR away from current price
Position sizeHigh ATR stocks need smaller position size to control risk

Example: AAOI price $157, ATR $14. ATR $14 is the average daily swing — on volatile days it can move $20+. Selling a call at $157 + $14 = $171 puts you right at the average, meaning it gets breached on roughly half of big-move days. Going 2 ATR out ($157 + $28 = $185) gives you a buffer for above-average swings, so you are not just safe on normal days but also on wilder ones. A stock like LMT with ATR $15 on a $607 price is actually calmer in percentage terms (2.5%) vs AAOI ATR $14 on $157 (8.9%).

6. IV Rank

Answers: Is IV high or low compared to the past 52 weeks?
Formula: (Current IV − 52w Low) ÷ (52w High − 52w Low) × 100

IV RankMeaning
0 to 20IV near yearly low — options very cheap historically
20 to 40Below average — still cheap
40 to 60Middle of the range — neutral
60 to 80Above average — getting expensive
80 to 100IV near yearly high — options very expensive

Example: A stock has IV low of 20%, IV high of 80% over the past year. Today IV is 65%. IV Rank = (65 − 20) ÷ (80 − 20) × 100 = 75. IV is in the upper quarter of its yearly range — options are expensive, lean towards selling.

Example: Same stock with IV at 25% today. IV Rank = (25 − 20) ÷ 60 × 100 = 8. IV is near its yearly low — options are very cheap, lean towards buying.

Needs 10 or more days of data to calculate.

7. IV Percentile

Answers: On what percentage of past trading days was IV lower than today?

20 to 40Cheap

More than 80IV higher than almost all past days — very expensive

Example: IV Percentile 85 means that out of the last 252 trading days, IV was lower than today on 214 of them. Options are more expensive than 85% of the time over the past year — strong case for selling premium.

Example: IV Percentile 10 means IV is almost at its cheapest in a year. Only 10% of past days had lower IV — great time to buy options before IV expands back to normal levels.

IV Rank vs IV Percentile: If a stock had one extreme spike last year, IV Rank makes everything else look cheap by comparison. IV Percentile counts actual days so one spike does not distort it. Use both — if both are above 70, options are genuinely expensive and selling premium is well supported.

Needs 10 or more days of data to calculate.

8. Earnings Play — Selling BEFORE Earnings sell-b4-earn

As earnings approach, IV spikes because nobody knows the outcome. Once earnings are announced, IV collapses instantly — this is called IV Crush. The strategy is to sell options when IV is inflated, then buy them back after the crush at a lower price, keeping the difference.

TimingWhy
7–10 days beforeSweet spot — IV near peak, still time to exit if trade goes wrong early
30+ days beforeToo early — IV has not fully inflated yet, premium is not worth the risk
1–2 days beforeToo late — full earnings gap risk with little extra reward
IndicatorWhat to look for
IV RankAbove 60–70 — premium is expensive, good to sell
IV/HVAbove 1.3 — market pricing in far more than historical vol
RSINeutral 40–60 — no strong lean, safer to sell both sides (strangle)
ATRSet strikes at least 2 ATR away from current price

Example: COIN reports earnings in 8 days. IV Rank is 82, IV/HV is 1.8, RSI is 52. COIN is at $180, ATR is $12. Sell a strangle: sell call at $180 + $24 = $204, sell put at $180 − $24 = $156. After earnings, IV crushes from 80% down to 45%. Both options lose value even if COIN moves $10. You buy them back cheap and keep most of the premium collected.

Risk: If COIN gaps up to $220 or drops to $140, the loss from the move exceeds the premium collected. Always use a credit spread (buy a further OTM option) to cap your maximum loss.

9. Earnings Play — Selling AFTER Earnings sell-aft-earn

After earnings, IV has already crushed and the stock has made its move. You are no longer playing IV crush — you are playing mean reversion. The stock has overextended and is likely to consolidate.

TimingWhy
Wait 1–3 days afterFirst day can still be volatile with follow-through buying or selling. By day 3 the new range usually stabilises.
Same day as earningsToo risky — stock is still reacting, direction unclear
ScenarioWhat to sell
Gap up + RSI above 70Sell call above the gap high — stock likely to consolidate
Gap down + RSI below 30Sell put below the gap low — stock likely to stabilise
Small move, RSI neutralSkip — IV is now low, premium is thin, not worth the risk

Example: MARA gaps up 18% after earnings. RSI hits 78. ATR is $3.50. Stock is now at $22. Sell a call at $22 + $7 = $29 (2 ATR above). Over the next 3 days MARA consolidates between $20–$23. The call expires worthless, you keep the premium.

Warning: After earnings, IV is low — premiums are thin. Only sell if RSI is extreme (above 70 or below 30). If it is not extreme, wait for the next setup.

10. Earnings Drop Premarket — Low Risk Same-Day Play same-day-earn

When earnings are announced before the market opens, the big unknown is already resolved. The stock has already gapped. You are now reading the reaction, not guessing the outcome. This is one of the better setups because you have a clear reference level to trade against.

StepAction
1. Wait 15–30 minDo NOT sell at 9:30am. Let the opening chaos settle. Watch if the gap is holding or fading.
2. Mark opening rangeNote the high and low of the first 15–30 minutes. This is your battle line.
3. Sell against the quiet sideIf gap up and holding — sell call above premarket high. If gap down and holding — sell put below premarket low.
4. Use credit spreadSell a call spread or put spread, not naked. Caps your max loss if the trade goes wrong.
IndicatorWhat to look for
Price vs premarket high/lowHolding above/below = gap strength confirmed, safe to sell against it
VolumeTapering off after open = consolidation coming, good to sell. Still surging = wait, move may continue
RSI at openAbove 75 after gap up = extreme, sell call. Below 25 after gap down = extreme, sell put
ATRUse today's ATR, not yesterday's — ATR will spike on earnings day

Example: DDOG reports strong earnings premarket. Stock gaps from $110 to $128 (+16%). Premarket high is $130. At 9:30am, RSI opens at 81, volume is high then starts tapering by 9:45am. Stock is holding above $125. You sell a bear call spread: sell call at $133 (above premarket high), buy call at $140. You collect $1.80 in premium, max loss is $5.20. DDOG consolidates between $124–$129 for the rest of the day. Calls expire worthless, full premium kept.

Avoid: Do not sell if the gap is less than 1 ATR — move is too small, premium too thin. Do not use same-day (0DTE) expiry on earnings day — gamma is extreme, a small move can wipe out the premium several times over. Use next week expiry minimum.

Decision rule: Gap holding above opening range high = sell call above premarket high. Gap fading back into yesterday's range = skip, direction is unclear, no trade.

11. Strategy — Cash-Secured Put (CSP) cash-sec-put

Sell a put below the current price and collect premium. If the stock drops to your strike, you buy 100 shares at that price. If it stays above, the put expires worthless and you keep the premium. Only use on stocks you genuinely want to own.

IndicatorWhat to look for
IV RankAbove 50 — premium is rich enough to be worth the risk
RSIBetween 40–60 — stock not in freefall
Strike1–2 ATR below current price, or at a strong support level

Example: HOOD at $22, ATR $1.20, IV Rank 62. You are happy to own HOOD at $19. Sell the $19 put, collect $0.60 premium ($60 per contract). If HOOD stays above $19 — keep $60, do nothing. If HOOD drops to $18 — you buy 100 shares at $19, but effective cost is $18.40 after premium. You own a stock you wanted at a discount.

12. Strategy — Covered Call cvr-call

You already own 100 shares. Sell a call above the current price to collect monthly income. If the stock stays below the strike, the call expires worthless and you keep the premium. If it rises above, your shares get called away at the strike — you still profit, just capped.

IndicatorWhat to look for
IV RankAbove 40 — collect decent premium
RSIAbove 60 — stock has already run up, less likely to push much higher
Strike1–1.5 ATR above current price, or at a clear resistance level

Example: You own 100 shares of MARA at $18, now at $22. RSI is 71, ATR is $3. Sell a call at $25 (1.3 ATR above), collect $0.90 premium ($90 per contract). MARA consolidates at $23 — call expires worthless, you keep $90. Sell again next month. Each month of selling calls lowers your effective cost basis.

13. Strategy — The Wheel wheel

A repeating income loop combining CSP and Covered Call. You are paid to wait at every step — whether the stock goes up, down, or sideways.

StepAction
1. Sell CSPCollect premium. Wait for expiry.
2. If assignedYou now own 100 shares. Move to step 3.
3. Sell Covered CallCollect more premium on your shares.
4. If called awayShares sold at strike. Go back to step 1.

Example: SOFI at $14. Sell $12 put, collect $0.35. SOFI drops to $11.80, assigned at $12. Effective cost: $12 − $0.35 = $11.65. Now sell a $13 covered call, collect $0.40. SOFI rallies to $13.20, shares called away at $13. Total made: ($13 − $11.65) + $0.40 = $1.75 per share — even though the stock went from $14 down to $13.20.

Best stocks for the Wheel: liquid options, IV Rank consistently above 40, stocks you are comfortable holding long term. Avoid stocks that can gap 30% overnight.

14. Strategy — Bull Put Spread bull-put-spd

Sell a put at a higher strike and buy a put at a lower strike. You collect the difference in premium. Your maximum loss is fixed — you cannot lose more than the spread width regardless of how far the stock falls.

IndicatorWhat to look for
DirectionMildly bullish or neutral — stock above a key support level
RSIAbove 45 — not in a downtrend
IV RankAbove 50 — collect enough premium to make the spread worthwhile

Example: GRAB at $4.50, ATR $0.22, RSI 53, IV Rank 58. Sell the $4.00 put, buy the $3.50 put. Collect $0.18 net premium. Max profit: $18 per contract if GRAB stays above $4.00 at expiry. Max loss: $50 − $18 = $32 per contract if GRAB drops below $3.50. Breakeven: $4.00 − $0.18 = $3.82.

15. Strategy — Bear Call Spread bear-call-spd

Sell a call at a lower strike and buy a call at a higher strike. Mirror image of the bull put spread. You profit if the stock stays below your sold strike. Maximum loss is fixed at the spread width minus premium collected.

IndicatorWhat to look for
DirectionMildly bearish or neutral — stock below a key resistance level
RSIAbove 65 — stock has already run up, less likely to push through resistance
IV RankAbove 50 — collect decent premium on both legs

Example: COIN at $185, RSI 72, clear resistance at $200, IV Rank 74. Sell the $200 call, buy the $210 call. Collect $1.50 net premium. Max profit: $150 per contract if COIN stays below $200 at expiry. Max loss: $1,000 − $150 = $850 per contract if COIN blasts above $210. Best case: COIN consolidates after a big run and the calls expire worthless.

16. Strategy — Iron Condor iron-condr

Combine a bull put spread (below current price) and a bear call spread (above current price). You collect premium from both sides and profit if the stock stays inside the range between the two short strikes. Best used on calm, range-bound stocks with high IV.

IndicatorWhat to look for
IV RankAbove 60 — both sides pay well, making the condor worthwhile
RSIBetween 45–60 — no strong directional trend
ATRRelatively low — stock is in a calm consolidation phase, not trending hard

Example: MSFT at $380, IV Rank 68, RSI 52, ATR $8. Sell put at $355, buy put at $345 — collect $1.20. Sell call at $405, buy call at $415 — collect $1.10. Total premium: $2.30. Max loss on either side: $10 − $2.30 = $7.70. MSFT stays between $355–$405 through expiry — you keep the full $230 per condor.

Avoid iron condors when a stock is in a strong trend or near a catalyst like earnings — one side will get run over.

17. Strategy — Diagonal Spread (Poor Man's Covered Call) diag-spd

Buy a deep in-the-money (ITM) call with a long expiry (6–12 months) — this acts like owning 100 shares but at a fraction of the cost. Then sell a short-dated out-of-the-money (OTM) call against it each month to collect premium. Same income as a covered call with much less capital tied up.

ComponentWhat to buy/sell
Long leg (buy once)Deep ITM call, 6–12 months expiry (LEAPS). Delta above 0.80.
Short leg (sell monthly)OTM call, 30 days out, 1–1.5 ATR above current price

Example: MSFT at $380. Buying 100 shares costs $38,000. Instead: buy a Jan 2026 $320 call (deep ITM) for $72 per share ($7,200 total). Each month sell a 30-day $400 call for $3.50 ($350 per month). After 6 months of selling calls: $2,100 collected, reducing your effective cost to $5,100. You control the same 100-share exposure with 80% less capital.

Best for: stocks with expensive shares (MSFT, NVDA, GOOGL) where buying 100 shares outright is too capital intensive. Requires understanding of delta and time decay on the long leg.

18. Smart Money — IV Skew

Every option has an implied volatility (IV). IV Skew compares the IV of out-of-the-money puts (below the stock price) vs out-of-the-money calls (above the stock price).

When institutions are scared of a drop, they rush to buy put insurance. More buyers = higher put IV. A big gap between put IV and call IV means smart money is paying up to protect against a fall.

Skew ValueWhat it meansYour action
Above +15%Institutions are heavily hedging downsideBEARISH — avoid selling puts, consider buying puts
+7% to +15%Mild fear premium, normal hedgingCAUTION — reduce position size on put selling
0% to +7%Normal market, no strong signalNEUTRAL — proceed based on other indicators
Below 0% (negative)Calls more expensive than puts — bullish speculationBULLISH — good environment for selling puts or buying calls

Example: NVDA at $900. OTM puts have IV of 65%, OTM calls have IV of 45%. Skew = +20%. Institutions are paying a 20% premium for downside protection. This tells you smart money is nervous about NVDA even while the price looks fine. Do not sell naked puts here — if institutions are hedging, they know something you don't.

19. Smart Money — Unusual Options Volume

Every stock has a normal daily options volume. When volume suddenly spikes to 2–3× its normal level, a large player (hedge fund, institution) has just placed a big bet. Retail traders don't move the needle — spikes almost always mean smart money.

The direction of the bet matters: if the spike comes with a put-heavy P/C ratio, they're betting down. Call-heavy spike = they're betting up.

2× to 3×Elevated — someone is positioningWatch — check if put-heavy or call-heavy
Above 3× + put-heavyBig money betting on a dropBEARISH — do not sell puts, consider buying puts

Example: COIN normal daily option volume is 80,000 contracts. Today it printed 280,000 contracts (3.5×) with a P/C ratio of 1.8 (heavy puts). Someone just spent millions buying downside protection or outright put bets on COIN. This is a strong bearish signal — step aside from selling puts until this resolves.

20. Smart Money — Dark Pool Short %

Most trades you see on an exchange are public. But big institutions often trade in dark pools — private exchanges where orders are hidden so they don't move the market while they're building a position.

FINRA (the US regulator) publishes a daily summary of short volume from these dark pools. Dark Pool Short % = what percentage of hidden institutional trades were short sells. High % = they are quietly betting against the stock. Data has a 1-day delay.

DP Short %What it meansYour action
Above 56%Institutions are net short in dark pools — quietly sellingBEARISH — avoid buying, consider defensive positions
44% to 56%Balanced — no strong directional betNEUTRAL — look to other signals
Below 44%Institutions are net buying — accumulating quietlyBULLISH — good backdrop for bullish strategies

Example: TSLA shows 61% dark pool short % for 3 days running, but the stock price is flat. Institutions are quietly shorting behind the scenes while retail sees nothing unusual. This is the "chef knowing the delivery is spoiled" moment — they're positioning before the news breaks. Pair this with a high IV Skew and you have two independent signals saying the same thing: get out of the way.

Note: Dark pool data is from FINRA RegSHO consolidated short volume files, updated each trading day with a 1-day delay.
